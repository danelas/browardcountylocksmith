Locked Out of Your House? Do This First

Published June 13, 2026

Stay Calm and Check the Obvious

Getting locked out is stressful. Before you kick the door or call anyone, take sixty seconds to slow down and think.

Check every door and accessible window. Many homes have a side door, sliding glass door, or kitchen window that was left unlocked. Don't assume—walk the perimeter. Make sure you didn't leave your keys in the car, at a neighbor's, or in a coat pocket you forgot about.

If you share the home with family or roommates, text or call them. Someone might be inside, or someone with a spare key might be nearby. If you have a spare hidden outside—under a rock, in a lockbox, or with a neighbor—now's the time to retrieve it.

If none of that works, move to the next steps.

Don't Force the Door

Forcing a door rarely ends well. Kicking in a solid wood or steel door usually breaks the frame, not the lock. You'll damage the strike plate, jamb, or casing—repairs that cost far more than a locksmith call.

Prying with a crowbar or screwdriver leaves visible damage and can bend the door itself. If you rent, you'll owe for repairs. If you own, you've just created a security gap until it's fixed.

Sliding glass doors and windows are fragile. Breaking one to get in means shattered glass, potential injury, and leaving your home unsecured until you can replace the pane. It's almost never worth it.

If you're desperate, call a locksmith first. The cost to open a locked door without damage is far less than the cost to repair what you break trying.

Verify the Locksmith Before Letting Them Work

Scam locksmiths exist. They quote a low price over the phone, show up in an unmarked van, then claim the job is "more complicated" and demand triple the price once your door is open.

Before anyone starts work, ask to see a business license and ID. A real locksmith will have both and won't hesitate to show them. Check that the company name on the van matches the one you called.

Get the final price in writing before they touch the lock. If they try to change the quote on-site without a clear reason—like discovering a high-security lock you didn't mention—walk away and call someone else.

Most reputable locksmiths will give you a firm quote over the phone based on your lock type and situation. If they won't, that's a red flag.

What If You Lost Your Keys?

If you're locked out because you lost your house key, the locksmith can do more than open the door. Once inside, you have two options: rekey the existing lock or replace it entirely.

Rekeying is faster and cheaper. The locksmith changes the pins inside the cylinder so your old key no longer works. You get new keys that fit the same hardware. This works on most standard pin-tumbler deadbolts and knob locks.

Replacing the lock makes sense if the hardware is old, damaged, or you want an upgrade—like a high-security cylinder or a smart lock. The locksmith can swap it out on the spot and hand you new keys before leaving.

If you're worried someone found your lost key and knows your address, rekeying or replacing is the right call. It's the only way to guarantee the old key won't open your door.

Prevent Future Lockouts

Once you're back inside, take five minutes to set up a backup plan. Hide a spare key somewhere on your property—not under the doormat or in a fake rock. Those are the first places a burglar checks.

A combination lockbox mounted to a fence post or tucked behind a downspout is better. Choose a four-digit code you'll remember but that isn't obvious (not your birthday or address).

Give a spare to a trusted neighbor or family member who lives nearby. Make sure it's someone you can reach any time of day.

If you have a smart lock or keypad deadbolt, use it. You can get in with a code instead of a key, and most models let you create temporary codes for guests or service workers. Just make sure you keep the batteries fresh—most smart locks will warn you weeks before they die, but only if you're paying attention.
